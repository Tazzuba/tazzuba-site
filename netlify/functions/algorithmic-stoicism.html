What Is Algorithmic Stoicism?

Algorithmic Stoicism rebuilds classical Stoic thinking — Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Seneca — as explicit if/then logic for the mind under pressure. Every incoming event runs through one filter: is it within my control? True, and it gets your attention and effort. False, and it gets released before it costs you anything. No event gets processed twice.

The Core Filter, In Practice

Take a real scenario: a client cancels the deal at the final hour. The filter runs the same way every time, regardless of how the moment feels:

MODULE — THE DICHOTOMY OF CONTROL

"Is this within my control?"

TRUE
Allocate resources. Act.
FALSE
Clear memory. Release.

This is the entire discipline in miniature: not suppressing the reaction to the cancelled deal, but routing it — instantly — to the branch that actually produces a result. Effort goes where it can do something. Everything else gets released rather than replayed.

No event gets processed twice.

Why "Algorithmic"?

An algorithm is a fixed sequence of steps that produces a reliable outcome regardless of who runs it or how they feel that day. That's the point: Stoic judgment shouldn't depend on mood, energy, or how well the day is going. It should run the same way every time — explicit logic, not willpower.

Common Questions

Is Algorithmic Stoicism a religion or a self-help trend?
Neither. It's classical Stoic philosophy — a 2,300-year-old tradition — rebuilt as explicit, repeatable logic. Closer to a decision-making system than a belief system.
Do I need to know classical Stoicism first?
No. The framework stands on its own, though familiarity with Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, or Seneca gives useful context for where the ideas originate.
How does this relate to Human Operations Engineering?
They're two layers of one system. Algorithmic Stoicism governs the mind — how you filter and respond to events. Human Operations Engineering governs the systems around you — the workflows and teams a leader is responsible for.
